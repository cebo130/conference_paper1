\begin{abstract}
Recommender systems frequently suffer from popularity and exposure biases, leading to a concentration of recommendations on a small subset of mainstream items and disparate accuracy across user groups. This empirical study focuses on two complementary views: \emph{macro-level} exposure inequality (how often items appear in top-$K$ recommendations) and \emph{user-centric} fairness (whether users with niche tastes pay a ``niche tax'' in RMSE or NDCG@10 relative to mainstream users).

Six recommendation architectures (collaborative filtering (SVD, ALS, $k$-NN), content-based filtering (TF-IDF, logistic regression), and a hybrid/ID-based random forest) were evaluated on three public benchmarks (MovieLens 1M, Last.fm 2K, Book-Crossing) and one e-commerce review corpus (Takealot). Matrix factorization models (ALS, SVD) behave as strong popularity amplifiers, with extreme Gini coefficients (e.g., $\sim 0.9998$ for ALS on Takealot) and very low catalog coverage, while content-based models spread exposure more evenly but often achieve weaker ranking accuracy in sparse settings. User-centric analysis shows a consistent accuracy gap for niche users under neighborhood-based models ($k$-NN), with dataset- and model-dependent signs for $\Delta\mathrm{RMSE}$. We argue that deployment decisions should audit both aggregate exposure metrics and segment-level accuracy, using ranking-oriented metrics when content models lack calibrated rating heads. A companion manuscript studies simulated feedback loops and retraining dynamics separately.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Personalized recommender systems drive discovery on digital platforms but are trained on interaction data skewed toward popular items. This skew yields \emph{popularity bias} \cite{abdollahpouri2017controlling}: algorithms over-recommend mainstream items and under-serve the long tail, homogenizing experiences and disproportionately affecting users with niche tastes \cite{abdollahpouri2019unfairness}.

Prior work often studies such biases on dense movie-rating benchmarks; such settings can under-represent the extreme sparsity and catalog scale of modern e-commerce. We present a cross-domain study that anchors MovieLens, Last.fm, and Book-Crossing against a large e-commerce corpus (Takealot), and we critique mapping content-similarity scores to explicit rating scales for RMSE, advocating ranking-based metrics (e.g., NDCG@10) where appropriate.

We address two questions: \emph{(i)}~Do algorithms concentrate exposure on already popular items, and how does this differ between collaborative and content-based families? \emph{(ii)}~Do users with niche interaction histories suffer a ``niche tax'' in RMSE or NDCG@10 versus mainstream users?

\textbf{Outline.} Section~\ref{sec:related} situates the work. Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes datasets, models, and protocols for macro and user-centric evaluation. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports results. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes. Simulated feedback with iterative retraining is analyzed in a companion paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Popularity bias and learning-to-rank have been studied extensively in recommender systems \cite{abdollahpouri2017controlling}; unfairness arising from popularity skew is discussed in \cite{abdollahpouri2019unfairness}. Standard benchmarks include MovieLens \cite{harper2015movielens}, HetRec/Last.fm-style data \cite{cantador2011second}, and Book-Crossing \cite{ziegler2005improving}. Implementations often use libraries such as Surprise for $k$-NN and SVD \cite{hug2020surprise}. Our contribution is a unified empirical comparison across domains, including a sparse retail catalog, with macro exposure metrics and user-centric segment analysis.

\subsection{Datasets}

We use MovieLens 1M (explicit ratings, genre metadata), Last.fm 2K (implicit counts mapped to 1--5 percentiles; tags and artists), Book-Crossing (explicit ratings; author/publisher text; implicit zeros removed), and Takealot (e-commerce reviews; sparse interactions; title, brand, department). A $k$-core filter ($\geq 10$ interactions per user and item) stabilizes public graphs. Table~\ref{tab:datasets} lists corpora.

\subsection{Models}

Baselines: user-based $k$-NN; SVD and ALS (matrix factorization); TF-IDF and per-user logistic regression on TF-IDF metadata; random forest on label-encoded user and item IDs. SVD and $k$-NN use Surprise \cite{hug2020surprise}.

\subsection{Protocols}

\textbf{Macro (top-10):} Gini on recommendation frequencies, ARP, catalog coverage, Spearman $\rho$ between training popularity and exposure, plus auxiliary diagnostics (extended panels, log--log and CDF plots) logged in the experiment run for analysis but not all reproduced as figures here.

\textbf{User-centric:} Users are split into Niche, Diverse, and Mainstream \emph{tertiles} by the mean popularity (in the training log) of items they interacted with. We report $\Delta\mathrm{RMSE}=\mathrm{RMSE}_{\mathrm{niche}}-\mathrm{RMSE}_{\mathrm{mainstream}}$ and NDCG@10. Content models without native rating scalars use a global training mean for RMSE; segment RMSE can be identical within such models; we emphasize NDCG@10 for cross-family comparison.

\section{Results and Discussion}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Macro-level bias}

Collaborative models concentrate recommendations on a few items (high Gini), while content-based models such as logistic regression on TF-IDF achieve higher catalog coverage. \textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:macro}} is the main macro summary: each cell is one model--dataset pair. Darker Gini cells indicate stronger inequality in how often items appear in top-$10$ lists; higher coverage means a larger fraction of the catalog receives at least one recommendation. Reading down the Takealot column, ALS is extreme on both axes (near-maximum Gini, near-zero coverage), whereas LogReg improves coverage substantially at lower Gini than ALS. The three public benchmarks (MovieLens 1M, Last.fm, Book-Crossing) are aggregated consistently; Takealot uses the official split and the same metric code. Table~\ref{tab:macro} gives two representative pairs (ALS vs.\ LogReg) to anchor the heatmap.

\subsection{User-centric fairness}

We summarize the niche--mainstream gap with \textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:fairgap}} only: bar height is $\Delta\mathrm{RMSE}=\mathrm{RMSE}_{\mathrm{niche}}-\mathrm{RMSE}_{\mathrm{mainstream}}$ for each model and dataset. Positive bars mean niche users have higher error than mainstream users under RMSE. $k$-NN shows a clear positive gap on Book-Crossing in our run; SVD on Last.fm can reverse the sign. Content models that use a global mean for every test prediction (ALS, TF-IDF, LogReg in our implementation) show \emph{flat} $\Delta\mathrm{RMSE}$ across models within a dataset; the bar chart should be read together with NDCG@10 from the tables in the artifact, not RMSE alone. On Takealot, NDCG@10 is uniformly low ($<0.02$) for all models, reflecting extreme sparsity; the small gaps in the figure are less informative than ranking metrics there.

\subsection{Synthesis}

Popularity bias is model- and domain-dependent. ALS can collapse to extreme inequality on massive sparse catalogs; content methods improve coverage but complicate RMSE comparison without calibrated scores. Systems should be audited for \emph{both} aggregate exposure (Fig.~\ref{fig:macro}) and equitable accuracy across segments (Fig.~\ref{fig:fairgap}).

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented an empirical framework comparing six recommenders across three public datasets and Takealot, measuring macro concentration and niche versus mainstream errors and NDCG@10. Matrix factorization can act as a popularity amplifier; content-based methods spread exposure but require careful ranking-oriented evaluation. Future work includes calibrated rating heads for content models, statistical significance across splits, richer user partitions, and, in a companion manuscript, simulated feedback loops with retraining.
