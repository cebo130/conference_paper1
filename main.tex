\begin{abstract}
Recommender systems frequently suffer from popularity and exposure biases, leading to a concentration of recommendations on a small subset of mainstream items and disparate accuracy across user groups. This empirical study focuses on two complementary views: \emph{macro-level} exposure inequality (how often items appear in top-$K$ recommendations) and \emph{user-centric} fairness (whether users with niche tastes pay a ``niche tax'' in RMSE or NDCG@10 relative to mainstream users). Six recommendation architectures (collaborative filtering (SVD, ALS, $k$-NN), content-based filtering (TF-IDF, logistic regression), and a hybrid/ID-based random forest) were evaluated on three public benchmarks (MovieLens 1M, Last.fm 2K, Book-Crossing) and one e-commerce review corpus (Takealot). Matrix factorization models (ALS, SVD) behave as strong popularity amplifiers, with extreme Gini coefficients (e.g., $\sim 0.9998$ for ALS on Takealot) and very low catalog coverage, while content-based models spread exposure more evenly but often achieve weaker ranking accuracy in sparse settings. User-centric analysis shows a consistent accuracy gap for niche users under neighborhood-based models ($k$-NN), with dataset- and model-dependent signs for $\Delta\mathrm{RMSE}$. We argue that deployment decisions should audit both aggregate exposure metrics and segment-level accuracy, using ranking-oriented metrics when content models lack calibrated rating heads. A companion manuscript studies simulated feedback loops and retraining dynamics separately.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Personalized recommender systems drive discovery on digital platforms but are trained on interaction data skewed toward popular items. This skew yields \emph{popularity bias} \cite{abdollahpouri2017controlling}: algorithms over-recommend mainstream items and under-serve the long tail, homogenizing experiences and disproportionately affecting users with niche tastes \cite{abdollahpouri2019unfairness}. As digital platforms grow in scale, the discrepancy between the "head" of the catalog (items with massive engagement) and the "tail" (items with few interactions) becomes increasingly pronounced.

Prior work often studies such biases on dense movie-rating benchmarks; such settings can under-represent the extreme sparsity and catalog scale of modern e-commerce. In dense datasets, collaborative filtering techniques have ample overlapping user histories to identify nuanced latent patterns. However, in sparse retail environments, the lack of behavioral overlap forces models to rely heavily on global priors, inadvertently amplifying popularity bias to extreme levels. We present a cross-domain study that anchors MovieLens, Last.fm, and Book-Crossing against a large e-commerce corpus (Takealot), and we critique mapping content-similarity scores to explicit rating scales for RMSE, advocating ranking-based metrics (e.g., NDCG@10) where appropriate.

We address two fundamental questions: \emph{(i)}~Do algorithms concentrate exposure on already popular items, and how does this differ between collaborative and content-based families? \emph{(ii)}~Do users with niche interaction histories suffer a ``niche tax'' in RMSE or NDCG@10 versus mainstream users? Furthermore, we aim to uncover how different algorithmic inductive biases--such as the localized neighborhood assumptions of $k$-NN versus the global dimensionality reduction of matrix factorization--contribute differently to the propagation of popularity bias across domains.

\textbf{Outline.} Section~\ref{sec:related} situates the work within the broader context of learning-to-rank and fairness literature. Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes datasets, models, the mathematical formulations of our chosen metrics, and protocols for macro and user-centric evaluation. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the quantitative results of our cross-domain experiments. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} synthesizes these findings, highlighting practical trade-offs for production environments. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes. Simulated feedback with iterative retraining is analyzed in a companion paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Popularity Bias and the Long Tail}
Popularity bias and learning-to-rank have been studied extensively in recommender systems \cite{abdollahpouri2017controlling}. The foundational work by Cremonesi et al. \cite{cremonesi2010performance} highlighted that while recommending popular items yields high accuracy metrics on average, it fails to help users discover relevant items hidden in the long tail. Steck \cite{steck2011item} demonstrated that popularity bias is not merely an artifact of user preference, but is actively amplified by standard collaborative filtering algorithms, which mathematically optimize for the dense head of the interaction matrix.

\subsection{User-Centric Fairness in Recommendations}
Unfairness arising from popularity skew is discussed thoroughly in \cite{abdollahpouri2019unfairness}. The concept of fairness in recommendation has evolved to encompass not just demographic parity, but algorithmic equity across behavioral segments. The ``niche tax'' describes the phenomenon where algorithms perform systematically worse for users whose tastes deviate from the global mainstream. Addressing this requires user-centric evaluation protocols rather than purely aggregate metrics. Standard benchmarks for testing these phenomena include MovieLens \cite{harper2015movielens}, HetRec/Last.fm-style data \cite{cantador2011second}, and Book-Crossing \cite{ziegler2005improving}. Implementations often use libraries such as Surprise for $k$-NN and SVD \cite{hug2020surprise}.

Our contribution builds upon this literature by providing a unified empirical comparison across domains, specifically introducing a sparse retail catalog (Takealot) to the analysis. By measuring both macro exposure metrics and user-centric segment analysis side-by-side, we bridge the gap between item-side fairness (exposure) and user-side fairness (accuracy parity). This framing follows broader multi-stakeholder recommendation concerns where user utility, supplier exposure, and platform objectives can conflict \cite{abdollahpouri2020multistakeholder}.

\subsection{Datasets and Preprocessing}

We use four distinct corpora to ensure our findings generalize across domains with varying degrees of sparsity and implicit/explicit feedback mechanisms.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{MovieLens 1M:} A dense dataset containing explicit 1-5 star ratings and rich genre metadata.
    \item \textbf{Last.fm 2K:} A music domain dataset where implicit listening counts are mapped to 1--5 percentiles to mimic explicit ratings. Rich tag and artist metadata are utilized.
    \item \textbf{Book-Crossing:} A highly sparse dataset with explicit ratings and textual metadata (author, publisher). Implicit zeros were removed to focus on explicit feedback.
    \item \textbf{Takealot:} A large, sparse e-commerce review corpus representing a real-world retail environment. Metadata includes title, brand, and department categorizations.
\end{enumerate}

To stabilize the user-item graph and ensure algorithms have sufficient signal to learn meaningful representations, a $k$-core filter ($\geq 10$ interactions per user and item) was applied to the public graphs. Table~\ref{tab:datasets} lists corpora.

\subsection{Evaluated Models}

Our evaluation spans multiple algorithmic families to isolate the effect of model architecture on popularity bias:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Neighborhood Models ($k$-NN):} User-based collaborative filtering calculating similarities via cosine or Pearson correlation.
    \item \textbf{Matrix Factorization (SVD, ALS):} Latent factor models that decompose the user-item matrix. The prediction rule for user $u$ and item $i$ generally follows $\hat{r}_{ui} = \mu + b_u + b_i + q_i^T p_u$, where $\mu$ is the global average, $b_u$ and $b_i$ are biases, and $q_i, p_u$ are latent vectors.
    \item \textbf{Content-Based Filtering:} TF-IDF vectorization on item metadata paired with cosine similarity, and per-user Logistic Regression trained on TF-IDF metadata to learn personalized feature weights.
    \item \textbf{Hybrid:} A Random Forest classifier trained on label-encoded user and item IDs along with basic metadata features.
\end{itemize}
SVD and $k$-NN are implemented utilizing the Surprise framework \cite{hug2020surprise}.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics Formulation}

\textbf{Macro (top-10):} To quantify the systemic skew in item exposure, we employ several aggregate metrics. The primary measure of exposure inequality is the Gini coefficient, defined over the vector of recommendation frequencies $x$:
\begin{equation}
    Gini = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} |x_i - x_j|}{2 n^2 \bar{x}}
\end{equation}
where $n$ is the total number of items and $\bar{x}$ is the mean recommendation frequency. A Gini coefficient approaching 1 indicates extreme inequality (a winner-takes-all dynamic), while 0 indicates perfect equality.

We also measure \textit{Catalog Coverage}, defined as the percentage of items in the catalog that appear at least once in any user's top-$K$ list. Additionally, we report the Spearman rank correlation $\rho$ between an item's training popularity (number of historical interactions) and its exposure frequency in the generated recommendations. Auxiliary diagnostics (extended panels, log--log and CDF plots) are logged in the experiment run for analysis but not all reproduced as figures here.

\textbf{User-centric:} Users are split into Niche, Diverse, and Mainstream \emph{tertiles} by the mean popularity (in the training log) of items they interacted with. We assess performance disparity by calculating the fairness gap:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta\mathrm{RMSE} = \mathrm{RMSE}_{\mathrm{niche}} - \mathrm{RMSE}_{\mathrm{mainstream}}
\end{equation}
Positive values indicate that niche users experience higher prediction errors than mainstream users. Because content models without native rating scalars use a global training mean for RMSE--causing segment RMSE to be artificially identical--we emphasize NDCG@10 (Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain) for cross-family comparison, which natively assesses ranking quality regardless of the underlying scoring scale.

\subsection{Macro-Level Bias and Exposure Concentration}

Collaborative models reliably concentrate recommendations on a few items (yielding a high Gini coefficient), while content-based models such as logistic regression on TF-IDF metadata achieve significantly higher catalog coverage.

\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:macro}} serves as the main macro summary: each cell represents a model--dataset pair. Darker Gini cells indicate stronger inequality in how often items appear in top-$10$ lists; higher coverage means a larger fraction of the catalog receives at least one recommendation. Reading down the Takealot column reveals the stark impact of data sparsity on collaborative methods. ALS is extreme on both axes (achieving a near-maximum Gini and near-zero coverage). In this environment, the matrix factorization model essentially collapses, outputting an almost identical top-$K$ list of best-sellers for every user.

In contrast, LogReg improves coverage substantially at a lower Gini than ALS. Because it relies on item features rather than collaborative overlaps, it can surface long-tail items as long as they possess relevant metadata. The three public benchmarks (MovieLens 1M, Last.fm, Book-Crossing) are aggregated consistently, but show less severe collapse due to their relative density. \subsection{User-Centric Fairness Gap}

We summarize the niche--mainstream accuracy gap with \textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:fairgap}}: bar height corresponds to $\Delta\mathrm{RMSE}$ for each model and dataset. Positive bars dictate that niche users suffer higher error rates than mainstream users--the literal definition of the "niche tax."

$k$-NN shows a clear positive gap on Book-Crossing in our run. Because neighborhood models rely on finding similar users, individuals with highly esoteric tastes lack sufficient nearest neighbors, resulting in poor estimations. SVD on Last.fm, interestingly, can reverse the sign, sometimes performing slightly better or equally for niche segments depending on latent dimensionality constraints.

As noted in Section \ref{sec:methods}, content models that fall back to a global mean for test prediction (ALS, TF-IDF, LogReg in our explicit implementation) show \emph{flat} $\Delta\mathrm{RMSE}$ across segments. Therefore, the bar chart must be interpreted alongside NDCG@10 metrics. On Takealot, NDCG@10 is uniformly low ($<0.02$) across all models (Table~\ref{tab:takealot_ndcg}), reflecting extreme sparsity and weak user-item overlap. In this regime, small RMSE gaps are less informative than ranking failures.

\section{Discussion and Practical Implications}
\label{sec:discussion}

The results of this study carry significant implications for the deployment of recommender systems in production environments, particularly for e-commerce.

\subsection{The Sparsity Trap in Collaborative Filtering}
Our findings confirm that Matrix Factorization acts as a massive popularity amplifier when applied to highly sparse datasets like Takealot. The combination of an objective function that minimizes global error and a lack of dense interaction overlaps forces the latent factors to primarily encode global popularity. In practice, deploying naive ALS on a sparse retail catalog may lead to a stagnant user experience, where the platform fails to leverage its long-tail inventory, thereby hurting long-term vendor fairness and user discovery.

\subsection{The Trade-off: Coverage vs. Precision}
Content-based methods (like TF-IDF and LogReg) naturally combat this by utilizing item attributes, yielding vastly superior Catalog Coverage. However, this raises a known trade-off: injecting diversity and coverage often degrades aggregate ranking accuracy (NDCG) in offline evaluations. System architects must consciously balance these metrics. Utilizing hybrid models or applying popularity-debiasing post-processing layers to collaborative models may offer a middle ground, yielding acceptable Gini indices without completely sacrificing the strong personalization signals of collaborative filtering.

\subsection{Evaluating Beyond RMSE}
A critical takeaway from our user-centric analysis is the inadequacy of RMSE as a standalone fairness metric when comparing disparate algorithmic families. The ``flat'' $\Delta\mathrm{RMSE}$ observed in non-calibrated content models masks true disparities in user experience. System audits must incorporate ranking metrics like NDCG@10 calculated at the segment level to truly understand if niche users are being underserved.

\subsection{Exposure Governance in Production Systems}
From a systems perspective, the practical question is not whether one model dominates all metrics, but whether the platform can govern a \emph{portfolio} of objectives over time: short-term relevance, long-tail discovery, and exposure equity. A robust deployment policy should therefore monitor a small panel of macro metrics (Gini, coverage, ARP) together with segment-level ranking quality, and trigger mitigation interventions when concentration rises beyond acceptable thresholds. This is consistent with recommendations from foundational recsys surveys and handbooks \cite{adomavicius2005toward,ricci2015recommender}.

\subsection{Why Head Dominance Persists}
The long-tail economics perspective emphasizes that many catalogs derive strategic value from discovery, not only hit-rate optimization \cite{anderson2004long}. However, if model training repeatedly optimizes on head-skewed logs, the system can converge toward a narrow recommendation regime. Quantifying inequality explicitly via measures rooted in concentration analysis (e.g., Gini) makes this drift visible and actionable for platform teams \cite{cowell2011measuring}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented an empirical framework comparing six recommender algorithms across three public datasets and one proprietary e-commerce dataset (Takealot). By measuring macro concentration alongside niche versus mainstream errors, we highlighted the multifaceted nature of popularity bias. We demonstrated that Matrix Factorization acts as an extreme popularity amplifier in sparse domains, collapsing to near-zero catalog coverage. Content-based methods effectively spread exposure but demand careful, ranking-oriented evaluation to ensure relevance is maintained.

Deploying equitable recommender systems requires auditing \emph{both} aggregate exposure (Fig.~\ref{fig:macro}) and equitable accuracy across user segments (Fig.~\ref{fig:fairgap}). Future work includes integrating calibrated rating heads for content models, establishing statistical significance thresholds across varied data splits, exploring richer user partitions based on demographic or temporal metadata, and investigating the long-term impact of these biases. In a companion manuscript, we further this research by simulating feedback loops and iterative retraining dynamics to observe how these static biases compound over time.
